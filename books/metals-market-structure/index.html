Metals Market Structure

The structure of precious metals markets.

Market Structure Series · Volume II

Metals markets are not a collection of isolated instruments. They operate as a layered system built on contract structure, liquidity hierarchy, session participation, and macro and industrial positioning.

Metals Market Structure presents a framework for understanding how the full metals complex functions, including gold, silver, copper, and thinner contracts. This volume examines how liquidity differs across metals, how behavior shifts between sessions, how metals interact with each other, and how regime changes alter pricing, volatility, and participation across the system.

Scope of this volume

This work begins with the structural role of precious metals in the broader financial system, focusing on gold and silver as primary instruments of liquidity, hedging, and macro expression.

It then develops a market-based framework, examining how futures activity, spot reference pricing, and institutional participation reshape volatility and directional behavior across metals.

From there, the analysis moves into structural drivers within the metals complex, treating each as a behavior class inside a connected system: gold as monetary metal, silver as hybrid monetary-industrial metal, and the broader metals environment as a reflection of positioning, macro sensitivity, and liquidity regime.

The volume culminates in structural relationships: how metals respond to interest rates, dollar strength, inflation expectations, and risk conditions, and how those pressures compress or expand across different market regimes.

Structural thesis

Metals behavior is driven by liquidity concentration, macro positioning, and regime-dependent capital flows. Gold and silver do not move as isolated instruments. They respond to structural pressures that repeat across environments.

This volume provides a framework for recognizing those pressures before applying any trading method.

Intended reader

This work is written for traders and researchers who approach markets as systems. It assumes familiarity with metals markets and focuses on structure, classification, and interconnection rather than introductory concepts.
